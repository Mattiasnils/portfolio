A love letter to whiteboards

If you've worked with me before, you know I'm a very digital being. I use my phone, computer and tablet for everything: taking notes, reading books, control my lights, monitor my sleep, track my runs and even prototype different furniture arrangements before moving to a new apartment.

Digital is great! I can access all of my work and media wherever I go, allowing me to live a relatively lean and flexible lifestyle with little attachment to physical possessions. Recently, I was basically able to move from Gothenburg to Stockholm bringing only a carry-on suitcase and a backpack worth of personal belongings. However, as my life has become more and more dominated by digital workflows, I realize there are still activities and tasks that benefit from being tactile in nature.

One such activity is collaboration.

Being in the same room and using physical artifacts to sketch, ideate, prototype and discuss, I feel, leads to much better engagement and flow in the team. I love the ability to quickly reach out and ask a question, point towards something and, above all, sketch together on a whiteboard.

I think whiteboards, more so than pen and paper, lend themselves perfectly to the iterative process of collaborative design work. The very nature of using erasable markers establishes a crucial sense of impermanence. Whiteboards are not bound by the rigidity of pixels or the permanence of ink; they invite experimentation, boldness, and the willingness to erase and start anew without hesitation.

The sheer size of a whiteboard provides just enough space and freedom for large visualizations and creativity, while still confining ideas and conversations to focus on the essential.

From intense brainstorming to abstract discussions, the canvas becomes our collective mind. Ideas flow, connections are made, and problems are solved in real-time. The whiteboard holds our wild scribbles, our flashes of genius, and our revisions with equal grace. When we gather around, markers in hand, we become a cohesive unit where individual thoughts merge into a shared vision that we can all see and build upon.

Beyond the practical aspects, there is something really satisfying about the tactility of drawing, erasing, and redrawing which connects us to our work in a way that digital mediums often cannot. The feel of the marker gliding imperfectly across the surface, the sound of ideas being born and reshaped — it's a sensory experience that grounds us in the present moment and fosters a deeper connection to our creative process.

As a digital being, whiteboards still hold a special place in my heart.
