Designing a Design System

For tactical interfaces

Summary

Researched and developed a foundation to a design system for complex Airborne Radar Applications.

Carried out multiple Ethnographic Studies and Co-design Workshops with developers, designers and end-users to gather requirements from different perspectives. Analyzed the current product portfolio through Interface Audits, mapped out inconsistencies and performed Heuristic Evaluations on the current UIs to identify usability and accessibility issues. Performed Literature Studies on how to bring coherence to UIs and read three books on best practices for building and managing effective design systems within large organizations. Finally, presented insights and ideas to decision makers across the company and prototyped a first iteration using Tokenized Components in Figma. This was followed by implementing parts of the system and providing to developers using components in Java Swing.

Designing a Design System for Coherent Tactical GUIs

Background

GlobalEye

GlobalEye is the world's most advanced Airborne Early Warning & Control (AEW&C) system. It provides long-range detection and identification of objects in air, at sea and over land by combining a variety of advanced sensors with a suite of sophisticated applications.

AEW&C Applications

Aboard the aircraft, operators use two applications, A1 and A2, for controlling and interpreting real-time data from the sensors. Data is transmitted via satellite to a third, ground-based application: A3.

Problem brief

The AEW&C applications have been developed by different teams in different locations without a synchronized design language. As a result, the UIs differ in visual style, interaction patterns and how tasks are performed, while also diverging from the Saab brand.

This leads to incoherence and usability issues for radar operators and impedes collaboration and resource sharing in development.

Project Proposal

To address these problems, the Human Factors Integration team at Saab posited that we might be able to design a unified interface to be used as a common base for increasing functional and visual consistency across the application suite.

Design Process

The task was approached using the Design Thinking Framework in order to better understand the context, its stakeholders and the root of the problem.

In practice, the project was divided into a pilot study followed by a main design process where the team iterated through the five steps of the framework

Pilot study

  • Interviews with designers, developers, operators and project owners

  • Application demos and trying the interfaces first-hand in simulators

  • Desk research on previous work, consisteny and coherence in UIs

  • Review of existing internal design and development tools and resources

Key findings
  • Developers and designers across project teams lack organization and platforms for communicating and coordinating efforts.
  • The applications have different UIs partly because they have distinct purposes. Hence, designing for consistency alone isn't the right approach.
  • The applications are developed in different programming languages, using different components and style guides.
  • Saab's existing Brand Portal and HMI Guidelines do not provide sufficient guidance for tactical UIs.
Stakeholder needs
  • Developers need a coordinated approach to application development, including better guidance in implementing design decisions as code.
  • Designers need more granular control and influence over holistic design decisions and how they are implemented across specific customer projects.
  • Saab's customers demand customized applications that can be tailored to their specific needs.
  • Radar operators need coherent UIs that support complex, focused work for extended periods of time in critical situations.
Coherence vs Consistency

Consistency is only concerned with uniformity whereas coherence expands the scope to consider how everything fits together in a meaningful and logical way to the user.

Minimizing the trade-off

The relationship between coherence and responsiveness to customer demands can be thought of as a trade-off curve. One way of minimizing the trade-off is using a design system.

Pivot

From understanding the organizational context, empathizing with stakeholder needs and researching methods for balancing coherence with responsiveness in UI design, it was concluded that a promising direction forward would be to pivot into designing a Design System.

Project scope

As design systems are complex and demand a lot of resources, the project was limited to the following deliverables:

  • A design system including foundational tokens, components and patterns.

  • A requirements list for how Saab may implement and manage the design system.

  • A reference UI in the form of a mockup exemplifying the utilization of the design system.

In essence:
A design system is a collection of reusable, coherent components and patterns built on fundamental rules and attributes which can be combined to build interfaces in an efficient, scalable and coherent manner.

Essentially, it functions as a platform - a common source of truth - that facilitates collaboration, communication and knowledge sharing within and between cross-functional product teams.

Operator
Contextual Inquiery

In order to better understand the context and how the applications are used in practice, a contextual inquiry was carried out with experienced operators in a simulator designed for A1 training.

Key insights
  • A1 and A2 are often used in contextual proximity, but the same operators can also be assigned to A3.
  • The work involves high cognitive load under extended periods of time, day and night.
  • Operators like to set up their interface according to personal preference and mission demands.
  • The work environment is noisy and dimly lit.

Defining the Design System

Interface Audit & Analysis

An interface audit was performed by deconstructing the existing UIs into components and systematically analyzing for similarities and inconsistencies. This allowed the team to identify components and patterns to include in the design system.

Co-design Workshop

In order to establish a common understanding and foundation for a design system for AEW&C applications, a remote workshop was held in Miro with two frontend developers and one UX Designer, each representing A1, A2 and A3.

Topics discussed:
  • A common component taxonomy.
  • What components to include in the design system.
  • What developers need to use and contribute to a design system.
  • How to enable responsiveness in the design system.

Ideation & Prototyping

Sketching

While discussing the general design system architecture and how tokens and components may be defined and presented in the system, sketching was used to visualize ideas and align the team on a common approach.

Prototyping in Figma

A first iteration of the design system was prototyped in Figma. Utilizing its native support for components and variants combined with the Figma Tokens-plugin allowed the team to iterate between different levels of the system in parallel in order to ensure that tokens could be combined into components, which in turn could be used to build coherent interfaces.

Testing & Feedback

Implementation test

To test the implementability of the design system, a workshop was held with developers of A1, A2 and A3. They were tasked with implementing a button component from scratch by following the component specifications and guidelines. This was followed by an open feedback discussion.

Key feedback
  • The prototype's structure and content was understandable and relevant.
  • Developers need even more guidelines and detailed component specifications.
  • Developers need explicit guidelines for how to specify the code.
  • It would be more cost effective and reliable to style preexisting components than creating a new component library from scratch.
Feedback presentation

As a design system involves a high level of teamwork and impacts the work for many stakeholders at Saab, it was critical to gain feedback and buy-in from across the entire company, including the executive group. Therefore, a presentation and Q&A was held with 50+ employees representing various teams and domains.

Key outcomes
  • The work was positively received across the board; from designers, developers, and product owners to managers, system engineers and executive staff.
  • The Q&A sparked discussions around ownership, contribution, organization, and how to balance coherence and responsiveness.
  • The discussions initiated the formation of a global, cross-functional project group that would continue the work by piloting a design system on the more coherent surface-based radar applications.
Implementation

From the implementation test it was clear that it would be difficult and time consuming to create a new component library from scratch. Therefore, it was decided that the design system should be implemented using preexisting JComponents and have a Look & Feel that allows for a high degree of customization. The FlatLaf Look & Feel was selected and combined with insights from the design research to create a new iteration of the design system with programmed token values and component styles.

Design System

Tokens
Spacing, Sizing & Shape

The Design System is built on a base unit of 4x4 pixels which is multiplied by a factor to create standard sizes, margins, widths, radii and grids. Tokens are then combined to form components.

Typography

The typography adheres to Saab's Brand Portal which specifices the Aktiv Grotesk font. It can be described as taking an authoritative but neutral position, supporting any message without overpowering it. The font is highly versitile and features 24 different styles.

Colors
Primitive colors

Primitive colors provide a range of fundamental shades that define how other colors within the design system are derived and combined. They serve as a backbone to establish and create new colors in a cohesive, flexible, and scalable manner.

Base colors

Background and foreground colors are consistent with the dark color scheme specified in Saab's Brand Portal. The dark scheme is well suited for tactical applications as it is easy on the eyes when working in dimmed lighting for extended periods of time. The neutral base colors also make vibrant status indicators more discernible.

Semantic colors

Semantic colors are colors that carry meaning. The semantic colors in the design system stays consistent with conventions for tactical applications. E.g. red should only be used for errors and critical alerts. Lighter and darker shades can be produced through a formula where lightness and alpha values are transformed in a systematic manner:

  • n shades lighter = (lightness + 6, alpha + 10%) * n
  • n shades darker = (lightness - 6, alpha + 10%) * n
Components
Component structure

Having defined colors, geometry, radii, border widths, and typography, all components are specified by referring to tokens.

In effect, large visual changes can be managed with ease to suit different customer requirements whilst maintaining a coherent interface overall. This helps in balancing the trade-off between coherence and responsiveness in terms of visual style.

Conclusion

The results

This project has resulted in

  • A design system including foundational tokens, components and patterns.

  • A requirements list for how Saab may implement and manage the design system.

  • A reference UI in the form of a mockup exemplifying the utilization of the design system.

The results are expected to

  • Improve collaboration and resource sharing within and between product teams.

  • Enhance overall user experience by improving usability and coherence for radar operators.

  • Achieve a balance between coherence and responsiveness to customer demands.

The results of this project constitute a foundation for further development. Subsequent iterations are needed to be carried out by Saab in order to develop, integrate, and maintain the system over time.

My takeaways
  • Developed expertise in designing and managing effective design systems within large organizations.
  • Gained hands-on experience collaborating with developers and implementing design decisions as code.
  • Learned to leverage Figma components, variants, properties, and plug-ins to create coherent, flexible, and reusable designs.
  • Aquired appreciation for complex software development in customer-driven projects with many stakeholders and constraints.
  • Gained a more holistic understanding of product development, considering how my design impacts different stakeholders.
  • Improved ability to independently plan and lead the design process, including method selection, time and scope management, workshop facilitation, user research, and addressing conflicting stakeholder needs.
